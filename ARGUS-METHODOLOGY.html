01

The thesis

Crypto due-diligence fails in three predictable ways: the people behind a project are anonymous, the connections between them are invisible, and the "facts" a subject presents are unverified. ARGUS is built to close all three.

It does three jobs at every layer: name the anonymous, putting a real-world entity on a wallet, handle, or developer; connect the disconnected, surfacing the deployers, funders, teams, and infrastructure that tie separate operations together; and prove or hold, verifying each claim against an exact fetched source or keeping it outside the decision. The output is never a black-box score. It is a reproducible investment case with receipts.

Table stakes

Get the founders, current roles, product, token or public security, major funding, and headline legal history right. A famous subject missing an obvious fact is a release failure.

The ARGUS edge

Show who controls the company, protocol, contracts, treasury, governance, and public narrative; connect capital, counterparties, insiders, prior outcomes, and contradictions; then show the strongest evidence against the conclusion.

Decision standard

Answer four questions in plain language: what is true, what is material, what could change the decision, and what ARGUS found that a normal web summary would probably miss.

Proof status

Implemented invariants are regression-tested. Brian Armstrong, Hayden Adams, Sam Bankman-Fried, and Jupiter remain live release gates. Generic-model comparison, semantic report diffs, and cost-ranked next steps remain explicit targets until measured.

02

How a scan runs

the pipeline, phase by phase

A person or handle audit streams through these phases live; a token or site scan runs the on-chain and web branches in parallel. An optional source failure records a coverage state instead of becoming subject risk. A core attribution, persistence, or scoring failure blocks publication.

Intake P0

Resolve the profile, handle-change history (rebrands), and a claim corpus from recent posts + keyword search over full history. Extract the self-claims - ventures, endorsements, advisory seats, promoted tokens - that everything downstream will try to verify.

Routing P0

Classify the subject - Founder, Project, KOL, VC/Investor, Agency, or Member - which decides the axes it's scored on and the specialist reports it earns.

Team & affiliations P1

Three angles in parallel: what the account is tied to, who has publicly named them, and the team named in their own posts - corroborated against an independent source (the project's follow-graph, an archived team page). A brand handle over a named team is not an anonymity flag.

Network expansion Network

Second hop: go into the subject's ventures and pull the people behind each, so the graph traverses subject → venture → its team - and cross-links when a venture's teammate is also a known associate.

On-chain & adverse Token · Adverse

Trace the deployer's funding, sweep for serial launches, screen for rug/drain/FUD complaints across the subject and every discovered project and associate, and flag any manipulation tooling they build or operate.

Contradictions Contradictions

Cross-reference every claim against the collected evidence for internal contradictions - a stated launch date that the on-chain history disproves, a claimed endorser who has never acknowledged the project.

Analyst scoring Analyst

The Claude analyst scores each axis from the assembled evidence, an adversarial pass tries to break the verdict in both directions, and the composite is governed by role with hard caps applied.

Finalize & graph Finalize

Validate every evidence link before writing, then persist the report, evidence rows, checks, and axis links in one transaction with exact replay equality. Record every entity into the shared trust graph with canonical keys, and reconcile the verdict against the graph - a hard tie to an already-failed subject overrides a clean headline.

03

What you can scan

Person / handle

An X handle or name → a full people-audit: identity resolution, affiliations, GitHub footprint, promoted-token performance (KOLs), portfolio track record (VCs), legal & sanctions screens, and the connection web.

Token / contract

A contract address → the on-chain forensic suite plus the project behind it: safety, holders, clustering, operator trace, deployer & bytecode, market intel, docs, and the team investigation.

Website / recon

A domain → fetch-or-render the site, resolve its official X account and team, link its infrastructure to other operations, and - when the site is a real token's homepage - route straight to the full on-chain report.

04

The method, step by step

how a subject actually gets resolved

The tools below are the instruments; these are the plays that run them. Each is the real sequence, with the source each step draws on.

Finding a project's team & founders

  1. Read the project's own team page - including a docs. subdomain - and extract the roster with Claude, since a Grok summary misses the JavaScript-rendered page.
  2. Reverse-mention search: project and partner accounts that ever named, tagged, or announced a person as founder or team. Co-founder announcements and "meet the team" posts are often years old, on the project's own timeline, so historical posts are searched too.
  3. Mine the account's own X posts for team members it names.
  4. Deep web + LinkedIn + primary-source search by name for anyone still name-only. Company team pages, filings, portfolio pages, repositories, and reputable reporting replace dependency on an expensive Crunchbase API.
  5. Corroborate before attributing: a team is only taken as the account's identity when the account itself vouches - its handle is among them, it links its own site, or its posts name them. A by-name match to a same-name project's team is dropped as a collision, not presented as identity.
  6. Enrich: resolve name-only members to their X / LinkedIn; a LinkedIn-corroborated leader lifts the project's identity to Confirmed. A brand handle over a public, named team is not an anonymity flag.

Resolving a person's real identity

  1. Pull the profile and follower / following graph; check handle-change history (rebrands) so old posts and mentions travel into the search.
  2. Assemble a claim corpus - recent originals plus keyword search over full history - and extract the self-claims: ventures, endorsements, advisory seats, promoted tokens.
  3. Discover affiliations from two angles: the person's own footprint (accelerator and portfolio pages, press, filings, and GitHub orgs) and reverse mentions (projects that announced them). Capture each venture's own X handle and domain, the canonical keys that bridge it to the rest of the graph.
  4. Corroborate each claim against an independent source, and resolve professional records to confirm or contradict a claimed background.
  5. Cross-platform sweep: prior handles and the same username across GitHub, Farcaster, Reddit, Telegram; and a Claude-vision check on the profile photo (AI / stock / celebrity / logo).

Tracing the operator behind a token

  1. Resolve the deployer and the wallet that funded its gas.
  2. Trace the funding up-chain to the topmost anonymous hub, recursing when a hub is itself funded by another anon wallet - and stop at a CEX, a known off-ramp.
  3. From that hub, trace forward to every sibling launch it seeded - the other tokens behind the same hand.
  4. Flag serial launches and dead tokens, name every wallet via Arkham, and score each for hacker / mixer / sanctioned exposure.

Reading a token's holders

  1. Pull the holder list and separate DEX / CEX / LP liquidity from private-wallet concentration - an exchange's holdings are not a rug.
  2. Cluster wallets funded from a common source and collapse them, sized as a single % of supply; detect bundled buys.
  3. Weigh creator holdings and LP-lock - a broadly-held mega-cap reads differently from a thin memecoin with a hidden top-10.

Proving two operations are one hand

  1. Every entity is recorded on a canonical key - an X handle, a domain, a wallet, a contract fingerprint, a dev email, an analytics ID - never a fuzzy name.
  2. When a new audit emits a key another audit already holds, they bridge to one node. The connection appears without anyone drawing it.
  3. A hard tie - shared deployer, funder, bytecode, or dev email - to an already-failed subject overrides a clean verdict; a shared person or domain warrants caution.

Verifying - or holding - a claim

  1. An added fact is first checked to exist on source (the GitHub resolves, the site loads, the token trades). A non-existent target is rejected.
  2. Then ARGUS tries to prove it belongs to the subject - e.g. the GitHub's profile links back to this same X. Proven → published live.
  3. Verified-but-unprovable → held pending human approval; on approval, ARGUS diagnoses why it missed the fact and proposes one concrete pipeline fix.

The trust graph

how audits compound

Every audit contributes its entities - people, projects, wallets, emails, domains, analytics IDs - to a shared graph. The engine is what turns a stack of separate reports into one connected map.

Canonical keys

An entity resolves to a stable identifier (an X handle, a domain, a wallet, a contract fingerprint) - never a fuzzy name - so "Deks" and "Deks Protocol" collapse to one node, and a person's founded project lands on that project's own token/site audit.

Bridging

When two audits emit the same key - a shared deployer, funder, dev email, GA property, or contract bytecode - they bridge to one node. The connection nobody drew by hand appears on its own.

Ring alerts

Every new report auto-checks its subject against the accumulated graph and flags any connection to an already-FAILED / AVOID subject, showing the exact entity that links them.

Verdict reconciliation

A hard on-chain or identity tie to a flagged subject overrides a clean headline - a shared deployer, funder, bytecode, or dev email with a failed subject is the same operation, regardless of how the contract itself scans.

10

Scoring & verdict

The score is a weighted read across role-appropriate axes, then governed by three disciplines so it stays honest:

Hard caps

Disqualifying findings - a honeypot, reclaimable ownership, a serial-scammer deployer, a non-transferable token - cap the score regardless of a clean market. A pretty chart never papers over an unsafe contract.

Legitimacy weighting

A capability a rug can't fake - real CEX presence, deep market cap - relaxes the softer caps, so a genuine listed project isn't failed for a mint authority it will never abuse. Hard caps stay hard.

Confidence & negative space

A coverage-based confidence indicator says how much of the verdict rests on verified data, and a "what we couldn't verify" section states the gaps outright. An adversarial second opinion stress-tests the verdict in both directions before it's final.

Full-clearance coverage policy

Final clearance requires every never-waive safety screen recorded (identity resolution, OFAC name screen, trust-graph connections, and the founder related-asset distinction) plus at least 75% of applicable governing checks recorded. An enrichment path a provider cannot serve no longer withholds clearance indefinitely; waived gaps stay disclosed, and an unrecorded safety screen always withholds clearance.

The analyst layer

a human in the loop

ARGUS is a force multiplier, not an oracle. Every report exposes the tools to check its work and improve it:

Scan methodology

A transparent checklist on every report - every check that ran, and its real outcome: passed, flagged, nothing found, skipped-and-why, or couldn't-verify.

Propose an edit

Add something the scan missed. ARGUS verifies it exists on source, then tries to prove it belongs to the subject - proven → published live; verified-but-unprovable → held pending your approval; non-existent → rejected.

Hard link

Manually bridge the subject to another entity. The target is verified, then wired as a canonical edge in the trust graph - it shows in the connection web and triggers ring alerts.

Ask the report

A grounded chat over a finished report - "why didn't you connect this to X?" - answered only from that report's own evidence, pointing you to the fix when a link is warranted.

Self-learning

When you approve a missed fact, ARGUS diagnoses why its scan missed it and proposes one concrete pipeline fix - the engine gets sharper every time it's corrected.

Release gates

what must work live

A release must be more useful than a generic ChatGPT or Claude scan. Unit tests prove invariants; the live collector must still find the adjudicated facts itself. Each named case must scan, run targeted repair, persist an immutable report, reopen without rescanning, and answer fixed questions from frozen citations.

Brian Armstrong

Coinbase cofounder, chair, and CEO; Fred Ehrsam; former Airbnb role; COIN as a public security rather than a crypto token; Base with no native token; and the Coinbase SEC matter kept separate from Armstrong personally.

Hayden Adams

Uniswap Labs founder and CEO; protocol launch; Labs, protocol, and Foundation kept distinct; canonical UNI contract and chart; GitHub footprint; and legal events attributed to the exact entity and current status.

Sam Bankman-Fried

Former FTX and Alameda roles, cofounders, founding and bankruptcy; historical or distressed FTT; seven-count conviction, 25-year sentence, and forfeiture; criminal findings kept separate from SEC allegations.

Jupiter

Jupiter Exchange, JUP DAO, contributors, and related products kept distinct; Meow and documented leaders; canonical JUP chart and contract; liquidity, supply, unlock, governance, treasury, concentration, and at least two non-social product-usage measures.

Recall

Find every must-have fact and at least 90% of adjudicated core facts. A critical false fact, entity-attribution error, stock-token conflation, silent known-data omission, or missing required chart fails the release.

ARGUS advantage

At least one live founder and Jupiter must surface a cited, decision-changing connection beyond a generic biography, plus the strongest credible evidence against the conclusion.

14

Principles

the rules that keep it honest

Never invent a founder

Names without a verified handle are shown but not audited, and a project's brand account is never treated as a person behind it.

Existence isn't proof

A verified thing that can't be tied to the subject is held, not published. The bar to go live is corroboration, not existence.

Nearby is not attributable

A partner, portfolio company, competitor, namesake, nearby executive, or another tenant on a shared host cannot lend its facts to the audited subject. Subject, predicate, value, qualifier, and status must form one bounded claim.

Absence is a claim too

A failed fetch is a coverage gap, never a confident "anonymous team." A blank batched search is unresolved, not a completed empty screen for every question. Absence artifacts remain inspectable but cannot count as positive support; they link to an axis only when they match an explicit gap.

Explain, don't just score

Every verdict traces to the evidence and the checks that produced it, making the conclusion reproducible and open to challenge.

The names lie, the keys don't

Entities connect on stable identifiers such as wallets, handles, bytecode, and emails, not fuzzy name-matching that can merge unrelated actors.

Prove the release, not just the code

Green tests establish invariants. Named live canaries must still find the facts, persist the report, reopen the same snapshot, and answer from frozen citations before a release is called shipped.

Get sharper on correction

Every approved edit teaches the pipeline why it missed and what to change, so the system compounds with use.
